FOSTER SCHOOL OF BUSINESS

OVERVIEW

A key learning experience during my undergraduate education was studying abroad in a month-long exploration seminar, offered by the Foster School of Business. This program was officially called, Business Morocco: Triple Impact Africa, but the course was titled: Intercultural Collaboration & Sustainability Entrepreneurship.

My role in this experience was a "foreign" student; I'm a U.S. citizen studying in a foreign country, making me the "foreign" student along with our class. This experience was valuable to me because as a student in a different region of the world, I was intellectually aware of acknowledging other cultures, ideas, and laws while enriching the cool sights that makes Morocco unique than the rest of Africa.

PERSONAL IMPORTANCE OF WORK

The purpose of the program was to explore the international business of agriculture in Morocco, which according to expert.gov: "agriculture contributes around 15 percent of Morocco’s GDP and largely determines the growth level of the economy as agricultural output is highly variable from year-to-year. The agriculture, fishing, and forestry sector employs about 45 percent of the total workforce with a similar portion of the population living in rural areas." 

Participating in this experience made me realize the importance of applicable concepts, such as the value chain, sustainability, and interpersonal communication. Prior to Morocco, I have never study abroad before, and upon my return, I saw the world in a slightly different way. In addition, we impacted the triple bottom line of a developing region in the High Atlas Mountains, the highest elevated area in North Africa.

TASKS ACCOMPLISHED

  • Visited multiple international agencies and businesses in Casablanca, Rabat, Fes, and Marrakech
  • Learned the history and culture of Morocco for a week at Al Akhawayn University
  • Presented our business proposal to non-governmental organizations (NGOs) 
  • Trekked on the High Atlas Mountains for 3 days
  • Met and negotiated crop strategies with regional leaders
  • Co-wrote a grant for NGOs to provide financial assistance to the developing region.

LEADERSHIP COMPETENCIES

  • Analysis: Before traveling, I attended several pre-departure sessions with my class, analyzing the current situations in Morocco and the High Atlas Mountains. In 2016, there were a number of agricultural problems in the High Atlas region: high altitudes, erosion, irrigation efficiency, and others more. Additionally, we found amazing insights as we toured business and NGOs like the U.S. Embassy and the International Center for Agricultural Research, where we would eventually come up with a resolution that would significantly improve the social, financial, and environmental aspects of creating business value. Simultaneously, it was important to analyze the Moroccan culture and their business practices, as it's essential to practice all of these findings when meeting with the local agricultural heads in the remote area of Amsouzart, Morocco.
​​
  • Scope of Competence: Most of my classmates were majoring in business administration, construction management, and/or environmental science. Additionally, I was an incoming sophomore at the time when the other students were mostly incoming juniors and seniors. I, on the other hand, had no previous knowledge of any of the terminology the other students spoke about during our meetings, so it was difficult at first to catch up with what our class was talking about, because upon initial arrival in Casablanca, there was so much information being thrown at me (as well as dealing with jetlag). Later on, the class was split into 4 teams, which essentially turned into a competition of presenting the best-case scenario for a certain crop that can impact their triple bottom line. My expertise in my group was to focus on the finances of our implementation plan, while the others focused on marketing, industry research, and crops grown near the developing region.
 
  • Idea-generation: Coming up with a "best-seller" crop was not easy, as it took my group and I about a week and a half to generate the idea of an ideal crop for the farmers in the High Atlas region. My group chose to present the "carob and carob honey" crops because they both were relatively resistant to extreme altitudes, they require comparatively-low costs of growing and valorizing the crops, ultimately resulting in an increase of profit margins for the farmers and their communities. Plus, carob and carob honey are fascinating products that pickup frequently in Europe and North Africa, which will hopefully soon become more common in the United States, if logistical and competitive barriers permit.
​​
  • Collaboration: Prior to presenting such ideas to NGOs and the farming association in Amsouzart, we spent about a week in Ifrane, collaborating theories and activities with a cohort of domestic and international students at Al Akhawayn University. These students came from unique global backgrounds, and were in collaboration us with in-lecture topics and a garbage cleanup event. These activities gave me the exciting opportunity to network with other non-UW students, as well as learning a little bit about Moroccan history and its current innovations in agriculture. This one-week experience led my class and I to gain intercultural communication skills, applying them when speaking to organizational officials and the farming association.
 
  • Conflict Negotiation: Throughout my study abroad experience, my class and I travelled on a charter bus with a couple of interpreters from Casablanca to Rabat, Meknes, Ifrane, Fes, Marrakech, and Imlil. From Imlil, our class participating in a 3-day trek to get to the village of Amsouzart. After the trek, we met with the representatives of the farming association, who wanted to seek an implementation plan for our "class crop". Prior to the trek, our class presented such crop ideas in Marrakech, which was later decided by the presentees to propose the carob honey crop and almond butter. In the past, the farming association was growing saffron, but the crop didn't valorize enough for the community to grow and improve agricultural conditions, especially of dealing with diversity in the workplace. Our language barrier, even with the interpreters, made it difficult to listen to all representatives while coming up with an overall plan. However, after several consecutive hours, we managed to collectively negotiate an action plan for carob honey and almond butter, and later wrote a grant to the University District (Seattle) Rotary on behalf of the farming association.

LESSONS LEARNED & FUTURE ORIENTED STATEMENT

Looking forward, I expect these past and current experiences to have me keep an open mindset when interacting with other cultures. I hope that my new global perspectives will lead me to more exciting adventures as far my curiosity takes me. I think the lessons I have learned as a student in the Business Morocco program has intrigued my fascination for bringing people together through intercultural collaboration, finding alternatives to solve modern-day problems in developing regions. This role has helped me soar to new heights, as well as exploring other opportunities offered by the university.
