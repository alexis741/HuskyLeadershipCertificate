Leaders Del Futuro

OVERVIEW
UW LDF
my story
A key learning experience during my undergraduate education was being a mentor of Leaders Del Futuro (LDF). LDF aims to assist students from migrant, undocumented, low income, and/or underrepresented backgrounds in achieving their goals through individual mentoring and tutoring related to a student’s intended career path.

In 2016-2017, I had the opportunity of getting paired with David Lobo, a pre-business major. Throughout the year, I met with him several times for academic and professional purposes. This experience was valuable to me because it was an approach to know my mentee personally, creating a strategy for his academic and professional aspirations.

PERSONAL IMPORTANCE OF WORK

I was a mentee my freshman year in LDF, and at the time as a sophomore, I decided to become a mentor for a new member in the group. In addition, I served as the active Secretary of LDF for 2016-17.

As a mentor, I learned the value of academic progress. Before this role, I was a mentee, where I was guided by an upperclassman, meeting with him several times during the 2015-16 year. Back then, I was on the track of applying to business school with my mentor, but my experiences as a mentee help shaped my mentoring strategies for David, who also wanted to apply to business school. In result, I created an academic, professional, and personal impact.

TASKS ACCOMPLISHED

  • Scheduled meetings
  • Discussed updates and current events
  • Occasionally met to study when we're not conducting mentorship meetings
  • Strategized and planned out academic interests
  • Led a professionalism workshop for all members

LEADERSHIP COMPETENCIES

  • Mentoring: There is a lot that goes on between the relationship of a mentor and mentee. We were first paired up as mentor and mentee because we originally had the common interest of applying to the undergraduate business major. I find this experience super interesting because at the time, we were both sophomores! It's unusual to be paired up with another UW student that is essentially in the same graduating class as you're in, but it was pretty cool because it was easier for me to personally connect with him even further, as opposed to connecting with a first-year student. The only difference between us was our academic progress prior to applying to business school, where he was beginning to take the first-year business courses while I was completing up the second-year of pre-requisites. Despite the differences and similarities, we both learned a lot from each other as we went about participating in LDF activities, including our membership meetings, social events, and even fundraisers.
​
  • Creating Change: Every time I volunteer, participate, or serve an organization or group, I never often realize how much of an impact I am doing by simply being a part of it. Creating change does not happen overnight; it takes almost every inch of your body to take initiative for even the smallest tasks. Collectively, I made my mark on David's experiences in LDF at a point where took away a few key points in his undergraduate career: the competence of his own academic progress, the increase in self-development he has gained every time we engage in mentorship meetings, and the confidence to step out of his social boundaries. All of his takeaways didn't just affect him, it also reflected a bit on me as his mentor, which gave me additional insight about how to create change for others in the future. For future reference, I might implement SWOT and personality analyses for future mentoring relationships.
 
  • Inclusion: Leaders Del Futuro was originally founded by students in UW's College Assistance Migrant Program (CAMP) in 2014. I heard about LDF through CAMP, and joined as a mentee in 2015. Fast-forward to 2016, the membership of the organization slowly started to change, and as a mentor, I was looking forward to this change of inclusion for other backgrounds who share similar experiences of underrepresentation. My role gave me the chance to step out of my comfort zone and become inclusive with not just my mentee, but with other members in LDF. During my two years, I used my networking abilities to co-create icebreakers at our meetings, "breaking the ice" for shy freshmen to introduce themselves while stating what their motivations and academic aspirations are. Even at our social events, I often check-in on others regarding comfortability, academic progress, and other personal matters.
​​
  • Personal Contributions: There are countless ways that I contributed for LDF and my mentoring relationship, but the one I'd like to point out is my professionalism. All of us come from different parts of the world, bringing to the table with different strengths, weakness, opportunities, and (hopefully not) threats. In addition to my mentoring, I showcased my professional tips and tricks to other LDF members by leading career development workshops during LDF meetings a couple times a year. With my evolving expertise in modern-day career preparation, I displayed my cover letter samples, as well as my resume and profile on LinkedIn. The purpose of this was to introduce members on the cognitive process about their experiences and qualifications, which leads to articulating their talents during their applications and potentially: interviewing opportunities.
 
  • Listening: Speaking to your mentee is one thing, but listening to them is another. I used to tutor K-12 students as a senior in high school, and I learned a lot by simply listening to my students. Applying that to LDF, I usually let my mentee take control of the topics at our one-to-one meetings because it gives them the space to first speak what's on their mind, following up with academic-related conversation. Throughout the year, he enrolled in business courses, and is currently in the process of applying to his desired major. Mentoring is not a one-way street of communication, it involves the mentor to also listen to what the mentee wants or expects to do, and it is my obligation to reply with honesty, backing up my arguments with my own personal path at the UW.

LESSONS LEARNED & FUTURE ORIENTED STATEMENT

Looking forward, I expect these past and current experiences to not only help myself, but also my [former] mentee and the rest of LDF. I hope that my new leadership roles will advance me to roles where I'll impact dozens of academically-driven students from all parts of the spectrum. I think the lessons I have learned as a mentor in LDF have motivated me to direct underrepresented students to their major and other academic interests through experiences, stories, and mentoring. As a former mentee, I was fortunate enough to be guided through my freshman year by my mentor on most academic matters. However, my [recent] role has helped me seek additional opportunities offered by LDF, UW CAMP, and the Kelly Ethnic Cultural Center (ECC).
